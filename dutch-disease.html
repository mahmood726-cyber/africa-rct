The Dutch Disease of Global Health

HIV Funding as Resource Curse — How PEPFAR/Global Fund spending crowds out cancer, cardiovascular, diabetes, surgical, and mental-health research across Africa

The Dutch Disease Analogy
In 1959, the Netherlands discovered a vast natural gas field. The resulting export boom caused the Dutch guilder to appreciate, making other exports uncompetitive. Manufacturing declined. Economists coined the term Dutch Disease: when a boom in one sector causes the rest of the economy to wither.

In Africa’s research ecosystem, PEPFAR/Global Fund HIV funding is the natural gas. Billions of dollars flow into HIV infrastructure, attracting researchers, building trial sites, and training coordinators — all focused on a single disease. Meanwhile, cancer, heart disease, diabetes, surgery, and mental health research struggle to compete for the remaining scraps of attention and capacity.
Dutch Disease Coefficient (DDC) = HIV trials / non-HIV trials
DDC > 2.0 = Severe Dutch Disease | DDC 1.0–2.0 = Moderate Dutch Disease | DDC < 1.0 = Healthy portfolio

HIV Dependency Ratio = HIV trials / total trials

What Happens When the Oil Runs Out?

PEPFAR Budget Plateau (2020–present)
PEPFAR annual funding peaked at ~$7B and has plateaued. Political uncertainty means funding could decline further. For countries where 34.4% of research activity is HIV-focused, a funding cut would not just reduce HIV research — it would collapse the entire clinical trial infrastructure built around it.

In economics, when the oil runs out, countries that failed to diversify face catastrophic recession. In African health research, the same pattern threatens: countries that never built NCD, surgical, or mental-health trial capacity will have nothing left.
The Paradox of Plenty
Like oil-rich nations that remain poor (Nigeria, Angola, Venezuela), PEPFAR-heavy countries may have more total trials but less diversified research portfolios. Non-PEPFAR countries (DDC: 0.65) often have healthier research ecosystems despite receiving less funding overall. The abundance of HIV funding creates a monoculture that is brittle and unsustainable.

Methods

ClinicalTrials.gov API v2 was queried for interventional trials across 15 African countries. For each country, we retrieved trial counts for HIV, cancer, cardiovascular disease, diabetes, surgery, and mental health. The Dutch Disease Coefficient (DDC) was computed as HIV trials divided by the sum of all non-HIV sector trials. Countries were classified as PEPFAR-heavy (10 countries receiving >$100M/yr) or non-PEPFAR comparators (5 countries). All queries and calculations are reproducible from the cached data file.
