Africa has only 177 vaccine trials registered on ClinicalTrials.gov, compared to 3,485 in the United States. Of Africa's vaccine trials, 99.4% are concentrated in a single country: South Africa. This extreme concentration raises fundamental questions about who benefits from vaccines tested on African soil.

2. The South Africa Monopoly

176/177
99.4% of all Africa vaccine trials are in South Africa
Why South Africa? Four structural factors create this monopoly: (1) GCP-compliant infrastructure built over decades of HIV/TB research; (2) a genetically diverse population useful for immunogenicity studies; (3) English-speaking regulatory and medical workforce; (4) established institutions (MRC, SAMRC, Wits RHI, CAPRISA, Aurum Institute) with FDA-audit-ready sites.

What This Means

The remaining 53 African nations share just 1 vaccine trials between them. Countries like the DRC (population 100M, endemic Ebola) and Ethiopia (population 126M) are virtually absent from the global vaccine trial map, despite bearing disproportionate disease burden.

Nigeria sits at the heart of the meningitis belt, has the world's highest burden of circulating vaccine-derived poliovirus, and faces endemic yellow fever. Yet with only 11 vaccine trials, it has fewer than South Africa by a factor of 16:1. Structural barriers include regulatory fragmentation (NAFDAC), limited GCP infrastructure, security concerns in the north, and the legacy of the 1996 Pfizer/Trovan meningitis trial controversy that eroded community trust in clinical research.

Phase skew: Phase 3 trials account for 30.1% of Africa's vaccine trials, significantly higher than the global average of ~25%. This confirms that Africa is disproportionately used as a testing ground for vaccines already developed elsewhere, rather than as a site for early-phase discovery and development. Phase 1 (first-in-human) trials remain concentrated in high-income countries.

The core question: When a COVID vaccine is tested in South Africa on thousands of participants, does Africa benefit? The participants contribute their bodies and risk, but post-trial access is not guaranteed. Meanwhile, genuinely FOR-Africa vaccines (malaria, Ebola, meningococcal) often face years of regulatory delay and pricing barriers before reaching the populations that participated in the trials.

9. Post-Trial Access: Are Vaccines Available Where Tested?

The access gap: Vaccines tested in Africa face systematic barriers to post-trial availability:
  • RTS,S/Mosquirix (GSK): Tested in 7 African countries, 2009-2014. WHO recommendation: 2021. First routine rollout: 2023. 9-year gap from trial completion to access.
  • R21/Matrix-M (Oxford/Serum Institute): Tested in Burkina Faso, Kenya, Tanzania, Mali. WHO prequalified: 2023. Priced at $2-4/dose, more accessible but supply-constrained.
  • COVID vaccines: AstraZeneca tested in South Africa. South Africa received vaccines months after HIC countries, with COVAX delays. J&J vaccine tested in SA, initially exported before local availability.
  • HIV vaccines: HVTN 702 (SA), Imbokodo (SA) -- both failed. Decades of testing on African participants without a successful product.
  • Ebola vaccines: rVSV-ZEBOV tested during 2014-2016 West Africa outbreak. Licensed by Merck. Access limited to outbreak response, not routine immunisation in endemic zones.
The pattern: Africa contributes participants, risk, and disease burden. The resulting vaccines are often priced for high-income markets first, with African access dependent on GAVI, COVAX, or African Union procurement -- adding years of delay and uncertainty.
