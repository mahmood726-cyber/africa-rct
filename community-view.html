Communities across Africa host clinical trials but rarely shape the research agenda. Of 783 interventional trials in Uganda, only 50.8% show meaningful community engagement markers. The majority of trials are designed abroad, funded abroad, and answer questions set by foreign investigators. Communities provide the bodies; the Global North provides the questions.

What "extractive" means: An extractive trial is one where the community provides participants and disease burden, but the research question, protocol, and intellectual property are entirely controlled by external organizations. Community members are subjects, not partners. They may never see the results or benefit from the intervention tested on them.

The cluster trial paradox: In cluster-randomized trials, entire communities are randomized, but individual members may not have meaningfully consented. A village chief's agreement is not the same as informed consent. When a community is randomized to receive a new malaria intervention, individual families cannot opt out of the environmental changes. Yet these trials are approved under the same ethical frameworks designed for individual-level research. The gap between community consent and individual autonomy remains largely unaddressed in African trial ethics.

6. What Genuine Partnership Looks Like: CBPR Models

Community-Based Participatory Research (CBPR) offers a framework where communities are genuine partners, not just recruitment pools. Key principles include: (1) community members co-design research questions; (2) local researchers hold PI positions; (3) results are fed back to communities in accessible formats; (4) communities retain some intellectual property rights; (5) capacity building is an explicit outcome.

7. From Subjects to Partners: The Way Forward

Recommendations for genuine community partnership:
  • Community Advisory Boards (CABs): Mandatory for all trials, not just HIV -- with real decision power over protocol modifications, not just rubber-stamping.
  • Benefit-sharing agreements: Pre-specify how communities will access successful interventions. No more "we'll figure it out later" promises.
  • Local PI requirements: At least one local investigator in a leadership role, not just as a "site coordinator" for foreign PIs.
  • Results dissemination: Community-language summaries of findings, presented in-person, not just buried in English-language journals.
  • Research agenda co-creation: Communities should help set priorities, not just respond to what foreign funders want studied. The Village Health Team model in Uganda shows this is possible at scale.
