Condition Colonialism Index
Africa carries 25.0% of the global cervical cancer burden but hosts only ~107 registered interventional trials, compared to 3,451 in the United States (which bears ~1.4% of the burden). CCI = 25.0 / 1.4 = 17.9x.

The Leading Killer

Cervical cancer kills more African women than any other cancer.

An estimated 120,000 African women are diagnosed annually, and roughly 80,000 die -- more than from breast cancer, more than from any other malignancy. The disease is almost entirely preventable through HPV vaccination and screening, yet Sub-Saharan Africa has the lowest screening coverage and HPV vaccination rates globally.

High-burden countries like Mozambique, Malawi, and Zimbabwe have age-standardised incidence rates above 38 per 100,000 -- roughly 6x the US rate of 6.2/100k. Despite this, they collectively host fewer than a handful of interventional trials.

The HPV Vaccination Gap

HPV vaccines exist and are highly effective, yet trial access in Africa remains separate from vaccination rollout.

We identified 9 HPV vaccine-related trial(s) with African sites on ClinicalTrials.gov. The gap between vaccine availability and clinical trial activity is stark: while GAVI has supported HPV vaccine introduction in multiple African countries, research on dose schedules, long-term efficacy in African populations, and integration with screening programmes remains critically underfunded.

Screening-related trials (VIA, HPV testing, Pap smear): 29 found in Africa.

The Mozambique/Zimbabwe/Malawi Crisis

Highest incidence, fewest trials

Mozambique, Zimbabwe, and Malawi have among the highest cervical cancer incidence rates on the planet -- yet they are clinical trial deserts. Women in these countries face the highest risk but have virtually no access to investigational screening, vaccination, or treatment protocols.

Sponsor Analysis

Of the 107 cervical cancer trials in Africa, 7 (6.5%) are led by African institutions. The remainder are sponsored by pharmaceutical companies, non-African academic centres, NIH/US government agencies, or NGOs/multilateral organizations.

NGO and multilateral sponsors (WHO, PEPFAR, Gates Foundation, JHPIEGO) play a distinctively large role in cervical cancer research in Africa compared to other disease areas, reflecting the public health and prevention orientation of the trial portfolio.
