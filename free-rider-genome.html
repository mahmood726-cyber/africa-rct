The Free Rider Genome: Everyone Sequences, Nobody Treats

Olson (1965) meets Hardin (1968): Africa's genetic diversity is a public good exploited without reciprocal clinical investment.

1. The Game Theory

The Free Rider Problem (Olson, 1965) + Tragedy of the Commons (Hardin, 1968)

A public good is non-excludable (everyone can use it) and non-rivalrous (one person's use doesn't diminish it). Africa's genetic diversity is the ultimate public good for genomic medicine:


THE PUBLIC GOOD

Africa has the highest genetic diversity on Earth. Every out-of-Africa population is a subset of African diversity. This means African genomes contain variants critical for understanding ALL human drug responses, disease susceptibility, and gene function.


• H3Africa: 50,000 participants, 30 countries

• 3,500 whole genomes sequenced

• 1200+ publications globally citing the data

• Data policy: Open access after embargo period

THE FREE RIDING

Global researchers download H3Africa data, publish papers using African genetic diversity, develop drugs informed by African variants — but invest ZERO in pharmacogenomics trials that would benefit African patients.


• Africa pharmacogenomics trials: 30

• US pharmacogenomics trials: 482

• H3Africa interventional trials: 0

• Free Rider Index: 24.3x

5. The CYP2D6/CYP2B6 Gap: Different Genes, No Dose-Finding

Africans metabolize drugs differently. CYP2D6 ultra-rapid metabolizers (20-40% of Africans vs 1-10% of Europeans) convert codeine to morphine at dangerous rates. CYP2B6*6 slow metabolizers (40-50% of Africans) accumulate toxic efavirenz levels during HIV treatment. CYP3A5 expressors (>70% of Africans vs <30% of Europeans) need higher tacrolimus doses after transplant. Yet there are 30 pharmacogenomics trials in Africa to optimize dosing.

6. BRCA in Africa: Triple-Negative BC Higher, Targeted Therapy Absent

African women have the highest rates of triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) globally — the subtype most resistant to standard chemotherapy and most in need of targeted therapies. BRCA1/2 mutations are found at significant frequencies in African populations. Yet precision oncology trials (BRCA-targeted, HER2-targeted, immunotherapy) are concentrated overwhelmingly in the US and Europe.

8. What "Fair Contribution" Looks Like

The Coase Theorem Solution: Property Rights Over Genomic Data

Ronald Coase (1960) showed that externalities can be resolved through well-defined property rights and bargaining. Applied to African genomic data:


1. Data sovereignty (establish property rights):

African biobanks should require "clinical trial reciprocity" — any researcher downloading African genomic data must contribute to a fund for pharmacogenomics trials in Africa. The H3Africa data is currently open access; conditional access would create the missing property right.


2. Benefit-sharing agreements (Nagoya Protocol for genomes):

The Nagoya Protocol on Access and Benefit Sharing already governs biological resources. Extend it explicitly to human genomic data: if a drug is developed using African genetic diversity data, a percentage of revenue funds African clinical trials.


3. PGx trial mandates (internalize the externality):

For any drug where CYP2D6, CYP2B6, or CYP3A5 variants affect metabolism, require African dose-finding studies as a condition of WHO prequalification. Currently 30 such trials exist.


4. Reciprocal data contributions (end free riding):

Every non-African genomic medicine trial that cites African-origin genetic data should be required to include African sites or fund equivalent African trials. Convert free riding into fair contribution.
