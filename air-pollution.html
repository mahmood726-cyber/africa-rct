2. The Invisible Killer

Three billion people worldwide — including 900 million Africans — cook their daily meals over open fires burning wood, charcoal, dung, or crop residues. The smoke contains fine particulate matter (PM2.5), carbon monoxide, polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons, and hundreds of other toxic compounds. A typical indoor cooking fire produces PM2.5 concentrations of 500 μg/m³ — 33x the WHO guideline of 15 μg/m³.

Who is affected? Women and young children bear the heaviest burden. Women do 90%+ of cooking in sub-Saharan Africa and are exposed for 3-7 hours daily. Children under 5 are strapped to their mothers' backs during cooking. HAP causes:

• Pneumonia: 237,000 child deaths/year globally (HAP-attributable)
• COPD: 120,000 African deaths/year
• Lung cancer, cardiovascular disease, low birth weight, cataracts
• Burns: open fires are the leading cause of childhood burns in Africa

3. Clean Cookstove Interventions

The engineering exists. Improved cookstoves, LPG stoves, solar cookers, and biogas digesters can reduce HAP by 50-90%. The Global Alliance for Clean Cookstoves (now Clean Cooking Alliance) has distributed millions of improved stoves. Yet we found 7 registered RCTs in Africa testing these interventions. The evidence base for what works in African households — considering fuel availability, cultural cooking practices, and sustained adoption — is essentially absent from ClinicalTrials.gov.

4. COPD Burden in Africa

COPD from household air pollution is the fourth leading cause of death in Africa, yet Africa has only 105 COPD trials vs 1,353 in the US (a 12.9x gap). The vast majority of African COPD is caused by HAP, not smoking — making it a fundamentally different disease requiring fundamentally different interventions.

8. Comparison: China and India Clean Cooking Programmes

India: Pradhan Mantri Ujjwala Yojana (2016)
Provided 100 million free LPG connections to below-poverty-line households. Coverage: ~70% of target population. Accompanied by clinical trials and air quality monitoring studies (3 HAP trials registered).

China: National Clean Heating Plan (2017)
Converted 35 million households from coal/biomass. Coverage: ~55%. China has 17 registered HAP trials.

Africa: No equivalent national programme. No registered trials.

10. WHO Guidelines

WHO Indoor Air Quality Guidelines (2014):

• PM2.5 guideline: 15 μg/m³ (annual mean)
• Typical African cooking fire: 500 μg/m³ (33x exceedance)
• WHO recommendation: Transition to clean fuels and technologies
• SDG 7.1 target: Universal access to clean cooking by 2030
• Current clean cooking access in Africa: 20%

At current rates, Africa will NOT meet the 2030 clean cooking target. The number of Africans without clean cooking access is actually increasing due to population growth outpacing intervention.
