Investigation
Africa Bears 24% of the World's Disease Burden. It Gets 1% of Its Clinical Trials.
A 65-project analysis of ClinicalTrials.gov reveals systematic extraction of African patients for global drug development — with almost nothing returned
Analysis based on 20,799 trials across 30 African countries | Full data at github.com/mahmood726-cyber/africa-rct

For decades, the global pharmaceutical industry has used Africa as a testing ground — enrolling its patients in clinical trials, collecting their data, and developing drugs they will never be able to afford. A comprehensive analysis of every interventional trial registered on ClinicalTrials.gov reveals the scale of this extraction for the first time: Africa, home to 1.4 billion people and 24 percent of the global disease burden, hosts barely one percent of the world's clinical trials when measured against the true global total.

The analysis, spanning 65 projects and drawing on live registry data, introduces the Condition Colonialism Index — a metric that divides Africa's share of each disease's global burden by its share of global trials. The results are devastating. Sickle cell disease, a condition that affects 300,000 African newborns annually and kills most of them before age five, has a CCI of 70.2 — meaning Africa bears 75 percent of the burden but hosts just 1.1 percent of global trials. Stroke scores 56.4. Hypertension — Africa's silent epidemic, with the world's highest prevalence at 30 percent — scores 46.7.

Perhaps the most striking finding is what is completely absent. Africa — the continent with the greatest genetic diversity on Earth, whose genome has been sequenced by international consortia and whose DNA data underpins precision medicine worldwide — has zero registered pharmacogenomics trials. Not one study testing whether drug doses should be adjusted for African genetic variants. Meanwhile, 600,000 Africans die each year from household air pollution caused by cooking fires, and there is not a single registered interventional trial addressing it.

"The system isn't broken. It was never built for Africa. Africa provides the bodies, the risk, and the data. The drugs, the patents, and the profits go elsewhere."

The analysis reveals a pharmaceutical industry that takes far more than it gives. Johnson and Johnson, with annual revenues exceeding 50 billion dollars and more than 2,000 global trials, runs just 16 in all of Africa — a ratio of 0.8 percent. Even among companies with more African presence, the pattern is extractive: most trials are Phase 3 mega-studies with 50 to 400 sites worldwide, where Africa contributes a handful of patients among thousands. In Uganda, these "ghost enrollment" trials account for 7 percent of the trial count but the country contributes an estimated 0.59 percent of total enrollment.

The analysis also reveals a continent divided. Francophone Africa — 276 million people across 10 countries — has 5.5 times fewer trials per capita than Anglophone Africa. Conflict zones are nearly invisible: Somalia has 8 trials for 18 million people, yielding one trial per 2.25 million — compared to one per 2,100 in the United States. Meanwhile, Rwanda, a country that emerged from genocide just three decades ago, has built a research ecosystem with 8.6 trials per million people — outperforming its neighbour Burundi by a factor of 8.6 despite identical population size and shared history.

Cardiology
Heart Failure in Africa Looks Nothing Like Heart Failure in Boston. The Evidence Base Doesn't Care.
41 trials for a disease that strikes Africans 20 years younger, affects more women, and has causes that barely exist in high-income countries

When a 35-year-old Nigerian woman develops heart failure six weeks after delivering her fourth child, her cardiologist reaches for guidelines written in Europe and tested in North America. The landmark trials that shaped modern heart failure treatment — PARADIGM-HF, DAPA-HF, EMPEROR-Reduced — enrolled minimal African participants. The drugs they validated may not work the same way in a body with different genetics, different comorbidities, and a different cause of heart failure altogether.

Africa has 41 heart failure trials on ClinicalTrials.gov. The United States has 1,855. But the numbers alone do not capture the problem. African heart failure is a fundamentally different disease: patients are younger (mean age 52 versus 75), more often female, and more commonly affected by peripartum cardiomyopathy, rheumatic heart disease, and endomyocardial fibrosis — conditions that barely appear in Western trial populations. THESUS-HF, conducted in 2012, remains the only landmark heart failure trial designed for African patients. One trial. For an entire continent.

Rheumatic heart disease alone kills an estimated 240,000 Africans annually. It is completely preventable with benzathine penicillin G — a drug that costs two cents per dose. There are two registered trials.

Paediatrics
A Child With Cancer in Kampala Has a 20% Chance of Survival. In London, It's 80%. There Are 3 Trials.
Childhood cancer in Africa is not a death sentence because the disease is different — it's a death sentence because the evidence was never generated

Burkitt lymphoma was discovered in Africa. It is one of the fastest-growing cancers known to medicine — but also one of the most curable, with survival rates exceeding 90 percent in high-income countries. In sub-Saharan Africa, most children with Burkitt lymphoma die. Not because the cancer is different. Because the treatment protocols, the supportive care guidelines, and the drug dosing regimens were developed and tested elsewhere, for different bodies, in different healthcare systems.

Across all of Africa, there are 3 childhood cancer trials registered on ClinicalTrials.gov. The United States has 1,657. The Condition Colonialism Index for childhood cancer is 553 — the highest of any condition measured. For every trial the world runs on childhood cancer, Africa gets one five-hundred-and-fifty-third of its fair share.

"The 80-versus-20 survival gap is not biology. It is a policy choice made by every funder, every pharmaceutical company, and every government that decided African children were not worth the investment."

Ethics & Justice
"Woe to Those Who Give Less Than Due": A Quranic Framework for Research Justice
The Mizan Index applies the concept of divine balance to measure clinical trial equity across 20 African countries

In Surah Ar-Rahman, the Quran describes a universe built on precise measurement: "The sun and the moon follow courses exactly computed. And the sky He raised and He set up the Balance — that you may not transgress the balance." If creation itself follows exact computation, argues a novel analysis, then so should the distribution of medical knowledge.

The Mizan Index — named after the Arabic word for balance, scales, and justice — scores each of 20 African countries across seven dimensions drawn from Islamic jurisprudence: Hifz al-Nafs (protection of life), Adl (justice), Istikhlaf (stewardship), Shura (consultation), La Darar (no harm), Ilm (knowledge), and Amanah (trust). Each dimension is scored from zero to 14.3, yielding a composite score from zero to 100.

The weakest dimension continent-wide is Istikhlaf — stewardship and sovereignty. Foreign institutions control the research agenda in most African countries, choosing what diseases to study, which populations to enroll, and where to publish results. African communities bear the risk of experimental interventions while having almost no say in what gets tested. This, the analysis argues, is the essence of the Quranic warning in Surah Al-Mutaffifin: those who take in full measure from people but give back less than due.

Economics
The Dutch Disease of Global Health: How HIV Funding Crowded Out Everything Else
PEPFAR saved millions of lives. But a causal analysis suggests it also created a research monoculture that left Africa unprepared for its NCD epidemic.

In economics, "Dutch Disease" describes what happens when a boom in one sector — typically natural resources — causes all other sectors to atrophy. The Netherlands discovered this after North Sea gas revenues caused its manufacturing sector to decline. In African health research, the "resource" is HIV funding.

PEPFAR, the largest global health programme in history, has invested over 100 billion dollars in HIV treatment and prevention since 2003. It has saved an estimated 25 million lives. But a difference-in-differences analysis comparing PEPFAR recipient countries with non-recipients reveals an unintended consequence: countries that received the most HIV funding developed the narrowest research portfolios. Zimbabwe's "spillover index" — the ratio of non-HIV to HIV trials — is just 0.11. For every 100 HIV trials, Zimbabwe has 11 for everything else combined.

Meanwhile, non-communicable diseases — cardiovascular disease, diabetes, cancer, mental illness — now cause more deaths in Africa than infectious diseases. The research infrastructure built by PEPFAR cannot easily pivot because the funding is disease-restricted. The question facing African health ministers is stark: what happens when the HIV money plateaus — as it already has since 2021 — and 42 percent of your research capacity is HIV-dependent?

Maternal Health
Nine Trials. That's What the World Offered the Continent Where Two-Thirds of Mothers Die.
Africa accounts for 66% of global maternal deaths. It has 9 maternal mortality trials on ClinicalTrials.gov — fewer than a single US teaching hospital.

Every two minutes, a woman in sub-Saharan Africa dies from complications of pregnancy or childbirth. Postpartum haemorrhage — uncontrolled bleeding after delivery — is the leading cause. It is treatable. Preeclampsia is the second. It is manageable. Maternal sepsis is the third. It is preventable with basic infection control.

Yet across the entire African continent, with its 700 million women and girls, there are 9 interventional trials addressing maternal mortality registered on ClinicalTrials.gov. The United States, where maternal mortality is a fraction of Africa's rate, has 738. The Condition Colonialism Index for maternal mortality is 82 — meaning that for every trial proportional to Africa's burden, the continent receives one eighty-second.

The Sustainable Development Goal target for maternal mortality — fewer than 70 deaths per 100,000 live births by 2030 — is now mathematically impossible for most African countries. Sub-Saharan Africa's rate stands at 542 per 100,000. Closing that gap requires evidence. Evidence requires trials. Nine is not enough.
