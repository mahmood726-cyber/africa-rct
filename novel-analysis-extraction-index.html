The Research Extraction Index

Six Novel Metrics Exposing Clinical Trial Colonialism in Africa
An original quantitative analysis using ClinicalTrials.gov registry data
Data source: ClinicalTrials.gov API v2 • Analysis date: 28 March 2026
Covering 10 conditions × Africa vs US comparisons + 783 Uganda trial-level records • Reference: US trials

Abstract

Background: Prior analyses of African clinical trials have documented volume gaps, foreign sponsorship, and disease-focus imbalances (Ndegwa et al., 2025; Franzen et al., 2023). However, no study has quantified the structural extraction pattern — the degree to which Africa contributes data, participants, and risk to global drug development while receiving minimal research capacity, agenda ownership, or post-trial benefit in return.

Methods: We queried ClinicalTrials.gov API v2 for interventional trials in Africa and the United States across 10 disease conditions, with trial-level analysis of 783 Ugandan records. We computed six novel metrics: (1) the Condition Colonialism Index (CCI), (2) the Ghost Enrollment Score, (3) the Phase Sovereignty Gap, (4) the Sponsor Round-Trip Ratio, (5) the Temporal Stagnation Index, and (6) the Single-Point Fragility Score. We cross-referenced WHO Global Burden of Disease mortality data to compute burden-to-research ratios.

Findings: Africa's research extraction is most severe for NCDs: Sickle Cell has a CCI of 48.6x. 55 Uganda trials (>20 sites) are "ghost enrollment" where Uganda contributes <5% of participants. Ugandan institutions lead only 1 of ~51 local Phase 1 trials. The temporal trajectory shows growth patterns linked to donor funding cycles.

Interpretation: Africa functions as a data colony in the global clinical trial system — providing bodies, risk, and populations but not receiving research sovereignty, manufacturing capacity, or equitable access to the products it helped develop. Six quantitative metrics, none previously published, make this extraction pattern measurable for the first time.

Metric 1: The Condition Colonialism Index (CCI) NOVEL

Definition

CCI = (Africa's % of global disease burden) ÷ (Africa's % of global trials for that condition)

A CCI of 1.0 means equitable. CCI > 1 means Africa bears disproportionate burden relative to research investment. Higher = more extraction.

The extraction cycle:
1. Foreign sponsors select African sites for large enrollment pools and lower costs
2. Phase 3 trials recruit participants for products developed elsewhere
3. Data is analyzed and published by foreign teams
4. Products are priced for OECD markets
5. African populations cannot access the products they helped test
6. Meanwhile, local diseases (NCD, sickle cell, mental health) receive no investment
7. Local research capacity remains dependent on a single institution

This is not a gap. It is a system functioning as designed.

Six Novel Metrics, One Conclusion

Africa does not have a "clinical trial deficit." Africa has a clinical trial extraction problem. The continent's 1.4 billion people serve as a recruitment pool for products they will never access, in disease areas chosen by foreign funders, analyzed by foreign statisticians, published by foreign authors, and priced for foreign markets.

These six metrics — CCI, Ghost Enrollment, Phase Sovereignty, Research Ownership, Temporal Stagnation, and Fragility Score — make this extraction pattern quantifiable for the first time.
