2. The Leapfrog

Africa missed the era of large hospital-based clinical trial infrastructure, but with mobile penetration at 81% across our 6 target countries, digital health trials represent Africa's best chance to build evidence capacity without replicating Western brick-and-mortar models.

The M-Pesa Precedent: Kenya's M-Pesa mobile money service launched in 2007 and now processes transactions worth over 50% of Kenya's GDP. It succeeded precisely because Kenya lacked existing banking infrastructure. Can mHealth trials follow the same pattern — leapfrogging rather than catching up?

5. HIV mHealth Dominance

An estimated 34.7% of Africa's digital health trials focus on HIV/AIDS. This reflects the massive PEPFAR/Global Fund investment in HIV, but creates a dangerous blind spot: as NCDs overtake infectious diseases as Africa's leading cause of death, digital health tools for diabetes, hypertension, and mental health remain critically under-researched.

6. NCD mHealth Gap

The NCD blind spot: Africa has 8 digital health NCD trials vs 282 in the US (a 35.2x gap). NCDs now account for 37% of all deaths in sub-Saharan Africa and rising — yet the digital health infrastructure being built is almost entirely HIV-focused. When the NCD tsunami fully arrives, Africa will have mHealth tools for ART adherence but nothing for hypertension management.

9. The M-Pesa Model for Health

Lesson from M-Pesa: Kenya's mobile money revolution succeeded because it was designed FOR African realities, not transplanted from Western models. Digital health trials in Africa should follow the same principle:

• SMS-first, not app-first — most phones are feature phones
• Community health worker integration — leverage existing trust networks
• Offline-capable — intermittent connectivity is the norm
• Swahili/Yoruba/Amharic — not English-only interfaces
• USSD-based — works on any phone, no data plan needed

10. Rwanda's Digital Health Strategy

Rwanda: Africa's digital health leader. Rwanda has invested heavily in national digital health infrastructure including:

• RapidSMS — nationwide maternal/child health monitoring via SMS
• Zipline drones — blood/vaccine delivery to remote clinics
• OpenMRS — open-source electronic medical records nationwide
• Babyl (Babylon Health) — AI-assisted telemedicine reaching 2M+ users

Rwanda demonstrates that small African nations CAN build digital health infrastructure — the question is whether this translates into registered clinical trials and rigorous evidence generation.
