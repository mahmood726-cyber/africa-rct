The Principal-Agent Problem: Whose Research Agenda Is This?

Jensen & Meckling (1976) applied to Africa's clinical trial system. When the agent chooses the research, the principal bears the risk.

1. The Theory

Principal-Agent Theory (Jensen & Meckling, 1976)

When one party (the agent) is contracted to act on behalf of another (the principal), their interests may diverge. The agent has information the principal lacks. In Africa's clinical trial system:


PRINCIPAL

African populations who bear disease burden, provide bodies for trials, and need treatments for their leading killers. The principal wants research that matches their health priorities: malaria, neonatal mortality, maternal health, NCDs.

AGENT

Foreign sponsors (US universities, NIH, pharma companies) who choose what to study, where, and how. The agent optimizes for FDA/EMA approval, publication impact, and global drug pipelines — not necessarily African health priorities.


Four measurable dimensions of misalignment:


• Agenda Alignment Score — correlation between local disease burden ranking and trial condition ranking

• Information Rent — % of completed trials that posted results (do agents report back?)

• Incentive Gap — ratio of Phase 3 (FDA pipeline) to implementation research (local needs)

• Voice Score — % of trials with community engagement / behavioral components

4. Information Rent (Results Reporting)

Information asymmetry is the core of agency theory. The agent (foreign sponsor) knows the trial results before the principal (African community). The "information rent" measures how much the agent withholds. A low results-posting rate means the agent conducts research, extracts data, and disappears — the community never learns what happened.

8. What Realignment Looks Like

From Agency Theory to Policy

Jensen & Meckling's framework suggests three mechanisms to realign agents with principals:


1. Monitoring (reduce information asymmetry):

Mandate results posting within 12 months of completion. Currently only 0.0% of Uganda's completed trials post results — the principal never learns what the agent discovered.


2. Bonding (align incentives):

Require sponsors to co-fund implementation research proportional to their Phase 3 activity. If you run 10 Phase 3 trials, fund 5 implementation studies.


3. Residual loss acceptance (empower the principal):

Build local research capacity so the principal becomes their own agent. South Africa's higher local sponsorship (0.0%) shows this is possible. Fund African PIs, not just African sites.


4. Burden-matched quotas:

If malaria is the #2 killer but ranks lower in trial priority, require funders to explain the mismatch. The Agenda Alignment Score provides a quantitative target: move from rho = 0.911 toward rho > 0.8.

9. Policy Recommendations from Agency Theory

• For African governments: Establish national clinical trial priority lists based on local burden data. Require foreign sponsors to demonstrate alignment before ethics approval.

• For funders (NIH, Wellcome, Gates): Allocate funding proportional to burden-trial gaps. Mandate community engagement in all sponsored trials.

• For PEPFAR: Shift from disease-vertical (HIV-only) to burden-matched portfolios. Uganda's trial landscape is 42% HIV while cardiovascular disease and NCDs rise unchecked.

• For WHO: Publish annual "Agenda Alignment Scores" for every country, making principal-agent misalignment visible and politically costly.

• For African researchers: Use these metrics to negotiate with sponsors. "Your proposed trial worsens our Agenda Alignment Score" is a powerful argument.
