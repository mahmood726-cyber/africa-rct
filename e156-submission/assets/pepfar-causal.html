PEPFAR Causal Inference

Did HIV funding crowd out everything else? Quasi-experimental evidence from ClinicalTrials.gov (2000–2025) across 15 African countries using ITS, Difference-in-Differences, and Synthetic Control methods.

Study design: We apply three quasi-experimental methods to test whether PEPFAR (launched 2003) caused a measurable change in non-HIV trial starts in recipient African countries.

Treatment group: 10 PEPFAR-focus countries (Uganda, Kenya, Tanzania, Mozambique, South Africa, Nigeria, Zambia, Malawi, Zimbabwe, Ethiopia).
Control group: 5 non-PEPFAR countries (Ghana, Senegal, Burkina Faso, DRC, Cameroon).
Pre-period: 2000–2003 | Post-period: 2004–2025.
Outcome: Annual count of new interventional trial registrations on ClinicalTrials.gov, decomposed into HIV and non-HIV trials for PEPFAR countries.

1. Time Series Overview

Annual trial starts for PEPFAR countries (total, HIV, non-HIV) and non-PEPFAR controls. The vertical line marks 2004, the first full year of PEPFAR implementation.

Key observation: Examine whether PEPFAR countries show a divergence in total trials (driven primarily by HIV growth) while non-HIV trials stagnate or grow more slowly than the counterfactual trend. If the non-HIV line falls below what the pre-PEPFAR trend would predict, this supports the crowding-out hypothesis.

2. Interrupted Time Series Analysis

ITS model: Yt = β0 + β1·time + β2·D + β3·D·(time − T0) + εt
where D = 1 for t ≥ 2004, and T0 = 2004 (Bernal et al., 2017).

β2 captures the immediate level change at PEPFAR onset.
β3 captures the change in trend (slope) after PEPFAR.

3. Difference-in-Differences (DiD)

DiD estimator: ΔΔ = (&bar;Y;treat,post − &bar;Y;treat,pre) − (&bar;Y;control,post − &bar;Y;control,pre)

If PEPFAR crowded out non-HIV research, ΔΔ should be negative — PEPFAR countries grew less (or declined more) in total trial activity relative to control countries, after adjusting for the common time trend.

5. Synthetic Control Method: Uganda

Method: Following Abadie, Diamond & Hainmueller (2010), we construct a “synthetic Uganda” from a weighted combination of non-PEPFAR countries that best matches Uganda's pre-PEPFAR trial trajectory (2000–2003). Post-2004, the gap between actual Uganda and synthetic Uganda represents the estimated causal effect of PEPFAR.

Donor pool: Ghana, Senegal, Burkina Faso, DRC, Cameroon.
Weights: Burkina Faso: 100.0%

7. Confounders, Limitations & Comparison with Literature

Key confounders to consider:
  • GDP growth: PEPFAR countries may differ in economic trajectories, which independently affects research capacity. South Africa and Nigeria have larger economies that support more trials regardless of PEPFAR.
  • General research expansion: The 2000s saw global research growth worldwide. Both groups benefit from this secular trend, which the DiD design differentially accounts for, but the ITS may conflate it with PEPFAR effects.
  • HIV epidemic severity: PEPFAR countries were selected BECAUSE of high HIV burden. High HIV prevalence independently motivates HIV trials regardless of PEPFAR funding — a classic selection-on-the-outcome problem.
  • Other health aid: Global Fund, bilateral aid, and Gates Foundation funding flowed alongside PEPFAR, making attribution difficult.
  • ClinicalTrials.gov registration bias: Registration became mandatory (ICMJE, 2005) mid-study period, creating a step change in visible trials that is unrelated to PEPFAR.
Comparison with Bendavid & Bhatt (2009): Their landmark JAMA analysis found that PEPFAR funding was associated with large, significant declines in primary care, nutrition, and other health services in recipient countries relative to non-PEPFAR comparators. Our trial registry analysis extends this to the research domain: if PEPFAR created institutional incentives to focus on HIV at the expense of other diseases, we would expect non-HIV trial starts to grow more slowly in PEPFAR countries — which is testable with the data above.

Key difference: Bendavid & Bhatt used DHS survey data on health outcomes (immunization, family planning). We use ClinicalTrials.gov registry data on research activity. The mechanisms differ: their finding concerns health service delivery crowding out, while ours concerns research priority crowding out. Both reflect the same fundamental resource allocation question.
Limitations of this analysis:
  • Ecological design: country-level aggregation masks within-country variation.
  • ClinicalTrials.gov does not capture all African-led research, especially studies registered on other platforms (Pan African Clinical Trials Registry, WHO ICTRP).
  • The pre-PEPFAR period (2000-2003) is short (4 years), limiting power for the ITS and synthetic control pre-period matching.
  • Non-PEPFAR control countries may not satisfy the parallel trends assumption (a formal test requires more pre-period data).
  • Trial registration dates do not perfectly reflect when research capacity decisions were made or when funding flowed.
  • The synthetic control uses only 5 donor countries, limiting the convex hull of possible synthetic units.
