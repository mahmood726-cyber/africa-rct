The Extraction Pattern: Pharma Mega-Trials CRITICAL

The majority of SCD trials listing African sites are global mega-trials run by multinational pharmaceutical companies. Africa appears as a handful of sites within 35–172 site global networks. Enrollment from African sites is typically a small fraction of global totals, raising the question: is Africa a genuine research partner, or a source of easily-recruited, treatment-naive patients?

Ghost Enrollment: When a 142-site global trial lists 3–5 African sites, those sites may enroll 2–5% of participants. The trial's design, endpoints, dosing, and analysis are driven by non-African populations. Africa provides bodies; decisions are made elsewhere.

10 terminated trials, 9 pharma-sponsored. Every termination was a decision made at global HQ based on non-African data (futility, portfolio reprioritization, or safety signals detected in non-African populations). African patients who enrolled lost access to investigational drugs, often with no alternative treatment available.

Zero terminations were due to African-specific safety signals. Every decision was made on non-African data for non-African commercial reasons.

The Hydroxyurea Story THE REAL SOLUTION

Hydroxyurea is the only disease-modifying drug on the WHO Essential Medicines List for SCD. It costs approximately $0.50/day. It reduces pain crises by 50%, reduces hospitalizations, reduces mortality, and may prevent strokes. It has been proven effective in 4 African countries (REACH trial). It is the single most impactful intervention available for the 75% of global SCD patients who live in Africa.

And it is the focus of the only genuinely Africa-designed research programs.

The Egyptian Pattern: Small-Scale Local Research UNDER-RESOURCED

Egypt contributes ~30 SCD trial records, but these are overwhelmingly small single-center university studies (10–60 patients) from Cairo University, Ain Shams, Alexandria, Tanta, and Zagazig Universities. They study locally-relevant questions: zinc supplementation, vitamin D, N-acetylcysteine, omega-3, L-carnitine, sildenafil for pulmonary hypertension, and hydroxyurea optimization.

These represent genuine local research capacity but are severely under-resourced: small sample sizes, single-center designs, limited follow-up. No Egyptian SCD trial exceeds 100 patients. Combined, they represent important hypothesis-generating work that deserves multi-center validation — but that validation funding never arrives.

8
The CCI Calculation: 47.7x EXTREME

The Condition Colonialism Index (CCI) quantifies the mismatch between disease burden and research investment:

CCI = (Disease Burden Share) / (Trial Share)

Africa's SCD burden: ~75% of all global SCD patients (est. 5–6 million of 7.7 million worldwide)

Africa's SCD trial share: ~1.6% (~80 of ~5,000 global SCD trials on ClinicalTrials.gov)

CCI = 75 / 1.6 = 47.7x

For context:

Region SCD Burden Share SCD Trial Share CCI Interpretation
Africa ~75% ~1.6% 47.7x Extreme under-research relative to burden
United States ~1.3% ~60% 0.02x Massively over-represented in research
Europe ~1% ~15% 0.07x Over-represented
India / Middle East ~20% ~8% 2.5x Under-researched but less extreme

47.7x is the highest CCI for any major disease-region combination we have calculated. For comparison: Africa's general CCI (all conditions) is approximately 3.8x. SCD in Africa is 12.6 times more neglected than Africa's already-neglected average. This is the world's most extreme research-burden mismatch.

The Paradox

Africa carries 75% of the world's sickle cell disease burden but hosts 1.6% of the world's SCD trials — a 47.7x mismatch.

Of the ~80 trials that do exist, roughly half are pharma mega-trials testing drugs costing $100,000+/year — drugs no African health system can afford, run by sponsors who have terminated 10 studies after extracting data from African patients.

The genuinely useful research — hydroxyurea trials from Vanderbilt, Cincinnati, Indiana, and a handful of African universities — amounts to 15–20 trials out of ~80. These test a $0.50/day drug that is already on the WHO Essential Medicines List.

The pharmaceutical industry uses Africa as a site of extraction: patients contribute to trials that generate data for drugs they will never access. Meanwhile, the proven, affordable solution — hydroxyurea — reaches only an estimated 5–10% of eligible African SCD patients due to health system failures that no pharmaceutical company is working to address.

Zero African institutions have led a Phase 3 new drug trial for SCD.

Zero terminated trials were terminated due to African-specific signals.

Zero pharma-sponsored SCD drugs tested in Africa are affordable in Africa.

The condition colonialism index for SCD in Africa is 47.7x. This is not a gap. It is a system designed to extract value from the sickest population on Earth.
