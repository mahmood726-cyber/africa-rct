The Spillover Failure

Key finding: PEPFAR-focus countries have built extraordinary HIV trial capacity (1,820 trials), but NCD research has NOT benefited proportionally (1,445 NCD trials, spillover index 0.79). The billions invested in HIV infrastructure — clinical sites, ethical review boards, data management systems, trained staff — remain siloed within HIV programs rather than being leveraged for the NCD epidemic that now kills more Africans than infectious diseases combined.

This pattern is consistent with what Hayes et al. (PMID 37643290) found in the PopART trial: despite building massive community health infrastructure across Zambia and South Africa, the systems were designed for HIV-specific endpoints and did not naturally extend to cardiovascular or metabolic disease screening.

The Non-PEPFAR Paradox

Paradox: Countries that did NOT receive massive PEPFAR investment may actually have more balanced research portfolios. Without the gravitational pull of HIV funding, these nations' (limited) research capacity is distributed more evenly across disease categories. Their absolute numbers are lower, but they are not structurally dependent on a single funder's priorities.

Policy Implications: Pivoting HIV Infrastructure to NCD Research

The window is closing. As PEPFAR transitions to "country ownership" and funding plateaus, the HIV infrastructure will either be repurposed or lost. The data above show that spontaneous spillover is NOT happening — active policy intervention is required.

1. Dual-mandate clinical sites

Require PEPFAR-funded clinical sites to run at least one NCD study per HIV study. The physical infrastructure (pharmacy, lab, data systems) is already in place.

2. NCD riders on HIV cohorts

HIV cohorts in PEPFAR countries already track thousands of patients longitudinally. Adding blood pressure, glucose, and lipid measurements costs marginal dollars but generates invaluable NCD data.

3. Ethics board cross-training

PEPFAR built institutional review board capacity that currently reviews only HIV protocols. Cross-training these boards for NCD trials removes a major bottleneck.

4. Data infrastructure sharing

Electronic data capture systems (REDCap, DHIS2) deployed for HIV programs can be extended to NCD registries at minimal cost.

5. Workforce transition planning

Clinical research coordinators, data managers, and community health workers trained by PEPFAR programs represent a skilled workforce that could pivot to NCD research — if transition funding is provided.
